# Classic Copy Examples

A swipe file of legendary copy to study and learn from.

## Headlines That Changed Advertising

### VW "Think Small" (Julian Koenig, Helmut Krone, DDB)

**Headline:** Think small.

**Why it works:**

- Counterintuitive in an era of "bigger is better"
- Radical honesty—acknowledges the car is small
- Minimal copy, maximum white space
- Turned a weakness into a selling point

*Lesson: Sometimes the boldest move is the simplest. Own your positioning.*

---

### VW "Lemon" (DDB)

**Headline:** Lemon.

**Body opening:** "This Volkswagen missed the boat. The chrome strip on the glove compartment is blemished and must be replaced..."

**Why it works:**

- Self-deprecating headline grabs attention
- Reveals rigorous quality control (1 in 50 rejected)
- Turns "lemon" into proof of quality
- Final line: "We pluck the lemons; you get the plums."

*Lesson: Admitting imperfection builds trust.*

---

### Rolls-Royce (David Ogilvy)

**Headline:** "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."

**Why it works:**

- Ultra-specific claim (60 mph, electric clock)
- Demonstrates benefit through concrete detail
- Not "quiet car"—proves it
- Borrowed from technical report (research pays off)

*Lesson: Find the specific fact that dramatizes the benefit.*

---

### Piano Course (John Caples)

**Headline:** "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano—But When I Started to Play!"

**Why it works:**

- Story hook creates identification
- Emotional transformation (embarrassment → triumph)
- Curiosity gap (what happened when he played?)
- Aspirational outcome (social victory)

*Lesson: Story headlines outperform claims. Show transformation.*

---

### Coat of Arms (Gary Halbert)

**Headline:** "Did you know that your family name was recorded with a coat-of-arms in ancient heraldic archives more than seven centuries ago?"

**Why it works:**

- Direct question creates engagement
- Flatters the reader (your family is special)
- Specificity adds credibility ("seven centuries")
- Curiosity drives them to keep reading

This single letter mailed 600+ million times.

*Lesson: Personalization at scale. Make the reader feel special.*

## Apple "Think Different" Campaign

**Full Manifesto (Steve Hayden, Rob Siltanen, Lee Clow):**

"Here's to the crazy ones. The misfits. The rebels. The troublemakers. The round pegs in the square holes. The ones who see things differently.

They're not fond of rules. And they have no respect for the status quo.

You can quote them, disagree with them, glorify or vilify them. About the only thing you can't do is ignore them.

Because they change things. They push the human race forward.

And while some may see them as the crazy ones, we see genius.

Because the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do."

**Why it works:**

- Positions Apple users as aspirational identity
- No product features mentioned
- Celebrates the outsider (Apple's positioning)
- Short, punchy sentences build rhythm
- Ends on empowering note

*Lesson: Brand manifestos sell identity, not products.*

## Avis "We Try Harder" (DDB)

**Headline:** "When you're only No.2, you try harder. Or else."

**Body excerpt:** "We're only No.2 in rent a cars. So why go with us? ... We can't afford dirty ashtrays. Or half-empty gas tanks. Or worn wipers..."

**Why it works:**

- Turns disadvantage into advantage
- "Try harder" implies better service
- List of specific negatives they avoid
- Honest, human tone

*Lesson: Position against the leader by reframing the competition.*

## Direct Mail Openings

### Gary Halbert Style

**Pattern interrupt + question:**
"What you are about to read is absolutely true. And, if you can spare three minutes, I'll explain how a $1 investment could legally put $20,000 (or more) into your bank account within the next 30 days."

**Characteristics:**

- Conversational tone
- Specific dollar amounts
- Time commitment stated
- Hint of legal concern addressed

---

### Joseph Sugarman Style

**Story opening:**
"I'm about to tell you a true story. If you believe me, you could be rewarded handsomely. If you don't, I'll make it worth your while to at least listen..."

**Characteristics:**

- Direct address
- Stakes established
- Curiosity loop
- Low commitment ask

## Email Examples

### Subject Lines That Work

**Curiosity:**

- "weird question..."
- "can I be honest?"
- "this might sound crazy"
- "I was wrong about [topic]"

**Benefit:**

- "[First name], 3 ways to [outcome]"
- "Your [problem] is solved"
- "The fastest way to [result]"

**Urgency:**

- "final reminder"
- "closing tonight"
- "[First name], don't miss this"

---

### P.S. Lines

The P.S. is often read first. Use it for:

1. **Restate the main benefit:**
   "P.S. Remember, you're getting [big result] in just [timeframe]."

2. **Add urgency:**
   "P.S. This offer expires at midnight Friday. After that, the price goes up to $497."

3. **Repeat guarantee:**
   "P.S. Don't forget—you're protected by our 60-day unconditional money-back guarantee."

4. **Add social proof:**
   "P.S. Already 2,347 people have joined. See what they're saying..."

5. **Create FOMO:**
   "P.S. I'm only accepting 50 people. 38 spots already taken."
