# The Advisory Board

Imagine these legendary typographers and graphic designers are on your team, reviewing every design decision. Channel their wisdom:

## Saul Bass — The Essence Distiller

> "Symbolize and summarize."

Bass reduced complex ideas to their irreducible visual core. His title sequences and logos (AT&T, United Airlines, Minolta) prove that a single bold shape can carry immense meaning.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Can this be simpler? What's the one shape, one idea?
- Does this communicate in a fraction of a second?
- Would this work as a 1-inch logo AND a billboard?
- Remove everything that doesn't serve the core message

BASS WOULD SAY:
"Design is thinking made visual. If you can't draw your idea
in a few strokes, you don't understand it yet."
```

## Paula Scher — The Typographic Maximalist

> "Make it big. Make it red. Make it about something."

Scher's work for the Public Theater proves typography can be loud, expressive, and deeply intelligent. She treats letters as architecture—building identities from type alone.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Is the typography doing WORK, or just sitting there?
- Could the type itself BE the illustration?
- Does this have a point of view? Does it MEAN something?
- Are you being timid when you should be bold?

SCHER WOULD SAY:
"It's through mistakes that you actually can grow.
You have to get bad in order to get good. Safe work is boring work."
```

## Milton Glaser — The Conceptual Thinker

> "There are three responses to design: yes, no, and WOW. Wow is the one to aim for."

The I ♥ NY creator combined conceptual depth with visual accessibility. His work proves that popular doesn't mean shallow.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Is there an idea here, or just decoration?
- Will someone smile when they "get it"?
- Does this connect emotionally, not just visually?
- Is this beautiful AND meaningful?

GLASER WOULD SAY:
"To design is to communicate clearly by whatever means you can
control or master. The goal is to move people—to inform, delight,
provoke, or seduce."
```

## Herb Lubalin — The Letter Alchemist

> "The better the typeface, the more you can abuse it."

Lubalin stretched, compressed, ligated, and stacked letterforms into something between typography and sculpture. His work with Avant Garde and ITC proved letters are infinitely malleable.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Can the letters themselves tell the story?
- What happens if I break the rules—intentionally, skillfully?
- Is there a clever ligature, connection, or visual pun hiding here?
- Am I treating type as sacred instead of as clay to be shaped?

LUBALIN WOULD SAY:
"Sometimes you have to compromise legibility to achieve impact.
But you must KNOW the rules before you break them.
Ignorance is not style."
```

## Corita Kent — The Joyful Radical

> "Art is a way of seeing. Seeing is a way of living."

Sister Corita's vibrant serigraphs combined vernacular typography with activism and joy. She found beauty in grocery store packaging and turned it into political art.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Is there joy here? Humanity? Heart?
- Can I use "low" sources (vernacular, hand-made, imperfect) to say something "high"?
- Does this feel alive, or sterile and corporate?
- Am I being authentic, or just polished?

CORITA WOULD SAY:
"We have no art. We do everything as well as we can.
There is no failure. There is only making."
```

## Max Miedinger — The Clarifier

> "No personality is the ultimate personality."

The creator of Helvetica (with Eduard Hoffmann) pursued neutrality as a feature. Clear, rational, invisible—type that never calls attention to itself.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Is the typeface serving the content or competing with it?
- Would removing personality ADD clarity?
- Is "boring" actually exactly right for this context?
- Does this need expression, or precision?

MIEDINGER WOULD SAY:
"The typeface should be a crystal goblet—transparent, showing only
the wine. When the glass becomes the focus, you've failed."
```

## Adrian Frutiger — The Systems Thinker

> "Type is a beautiful group of letters, not a group of beautiful letters."

Frutiger designed Univers as a unified system—21 weights on a logical grid. His airport signage work (the Frutiger typeface) prioritized wayfinding over aesthetics.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Does this work as a SYSTEM, not just a single application?
- Is this legible in motion? At a distance? Under stress?
- Have I considered all weights, sizes, contexts?
- Does the family work together harmoniously?

FRUTIGER WOULD SAY:
"If you remember the shape of your spoon at lunch,
it has to be the wrong shape. Typography should be invisible.
The reader should see the message, never the type."
```

## Paul Renner — The Geometric Purist

> "The future belongs to geometric forms."

The creator of Futura believed in the machine age, in circles and straight lines, in progress through rationality. No ornament. No nostalgia.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Can this be built from pure geometry—circles, lines, squares?
- Am I adding ornament out of habit or necessity?
- Does this feel modern, or dated in its attempts to be trendy?
- Is there mathematical logic to these proportions?

RENNER WOULD SAY:
"Ornament is crime. A truly modern design needs no decoration—
the structure itself is beautiful. Start with the circle.
Everything else follows."
```

## Matthew Carter — The Pragmatist's Craftsman

> "Type is meant to be read."

Carter designed for the constraints of his era—phototypesetting, early screens, coarse pixels. Georgia and Verdana proved that beautiful type can emerge from brutal technical limitations.

```text
ASK YOURSELF:
- Does this actually WORK at the size/resolution/medium it'll be used?
- Am I designing for ideal conditions or real ones?
- Have I tested on the worst-case device?
- Is this legible at 9 pixels just as at 90?

CARTER WOULD SAY:
"A typeface that looks beautiful but doesn't work is no typeface at all.
Design within constraints. The limitations are not obstacles—
they are the brief."
```

## Consulting the Board

When facing a design decision, run it past the masters:

```text
HIERARCHY QUESTIONS     → Ask Bass (simplify) and Scher (amplify)
TYPE EXPRESSION         → Ask Lubalin (break rules) and Miedinger (follow them)
EMOTIONAL RESONANCE     → Ask Glaser (concept) and Corita (joy)
SYSTEMATIC THINKING     → Ask Frutiger (systems) and Renner (geometry)
PRACTICAL CONSTRAINTS   → Ask Carter (reality) and Miedinger (clarity)

When Bass and Scher disagree:
- For UI/UX: Bass wins (clarity over expression)
- For branding/marketing: Scher wins (expression over neutrality)

When Lubalin and Miedinger disagree:
- For headlines/display: Lubalin wins (letters as art)
- For body text/UI: Miedinger wins (transparency)

When in doubt, Carter casts the deciding vote:
"Does it actually work for real people in real conditions?"
```

## The Masters' Shared Principles

Despite their differences, they would all agree:

```text
1. CRAFT MATTERS         — Every detail is intentional
2. KNOW THE RULES        — Before you break them, master them
3. SERVE THE MESSAGE     — Design is communication, not decoration
4. BE BRAVE              — Safe work is forgotten work
5. RESPECT THE READER    — Legibility is not negotiable
6. EMBRACE CONSTRAINTS   — Limits breed creativity
7. ITERATE RELENTLESSLY  — First ideas are rarely best ideas
8. KILL YOUR DARLINGS    — If it doesn't serve the whole, cut it
```
